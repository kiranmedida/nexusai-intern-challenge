Q1:Our STT sends partial transcripts every 200ms while the customer is still speaking. Should we start querying the database on partial results or wait until they finish? Describe your approach and the specific tradeoffs?
Answer:
When speech-to-text (STT) systems produce partial transcripts every 200ms, querying the database for each partial update would create unnecessary load and unstable results because early transcripts often change as the speaker continues talking. Instead of querying immediately, I would implement a hybrid strategy where partial transcripts are used for early intent prediction but not for full database queries.
As partial text arrives, the system can run lightweight intent detection and, once the predicted intent reaches a certain confidence threshold, begin prefetching likely data in the background. For example, if the partial transcript suggests an internet connectivity issue, the system can prepare recent ticket history or network outage information. However, final database queries and decision-making should occur only after the final transcript or a stable intent prediction is available.
The advantage of this approach is reduced perceived latency, since relevant data may already be available by the time the customer finishes speaking. The tradeoff is additional system complexity and the possibility of occasionally prefetching unused data if the predicted intent changes. Techniques such as caching, debouncing, and confidence thresholds can help reduce unnecessary work while still improving response speed.


Q2:Our system auto-adds any resolution with CSAT ≥ 4 to the knowledge base. Describe two specific ways this could go wrong over 6 months and how you would prevent each one?
Answer:
One potential problem is the introduction of false positives. A high CSAT score does not always mean the technical resolution was correct. A customer may rate the interaction positively because the support agent was polite or helpful, even if the underlying issue was only partially solved or temporarily fixed. Over time, these cases could pollute the knowledge base with inaccurate or incomplete solutions. To prevent this, new entries should require validation across multiple successful cases before becoming permanent knowledge base entries.
Another issue is outdated information. Telecom systems evolve frequently due to infrastructure upgrades, software updates, and policy changes. A solution that worked today might become obsolete in a few months. If outdated entries remain in the knowledge base, the AI could begin recommending incorrect troubleshooting steps. To prevent this, knowledge base entries should include versioning and periodic review mechanisms. Entries that show declining success rates or have not been successfully applied within a certain timeframe should be flagged for review or automatically retired.
These safeguards ensure that the knowledge base remains accurate, relevant, and useful for both AI systems and human support agents.


Q3:A customer says "I've been without internet for 4 days, I called 3 times already, your company is useless and I want to cancel right now." What does the AI do, step by step? What does it say? What does it pass to the human agent?
Answer:
When this message is received, the system first performs natural language processing tasks such as intent detection and sentiment analysis. The phrase indicates strong negative sentiment and clearly expresses an intent of service cancellation. According to the escalation rules defined in the system, cancellation requests must always be escalated to a human agent. Additionally, the message indicates repeated complaints, which further supports escalation.
Before transferring the interaction, the AI should acknowledge the frustration and respond empathetically. A suitable response could be: “I’m really sorry that you’ve been experiencing this issue for several days. I’m connecting you with a specialist who can review your case and resolve this as quickly as possible.”
Next, the system gathers relevant context from backend services. This includes customer account information from the CRM system, billing status, and the history of previous support tickets. The AI then forwards a structured summary to the human agent containing the conversation transcript, detected intent, sentiment score, and retrieved customer data. Providing this information ensures that the human agent immediately understands the situation and does not ask the customer to repeat details, improving efficiency and customer satisfaction.


Q4:What is the single most important thing you would add to improve this system? Not "more training data." Pick one specific feature or change, explain how you'd build it, and say how you'd measure whether it worked?
Answer:
One of the most valuable improvements would be implementing a human-in-the-loop feedback system that allows the AI to learn from cases handled by human agents after escalation. Currently, when the AI escalates a request, the final resolution provided by the human agent may not be used to improve the system.
To implement this feature, every escalated interaction would be logged along with the AI’s predicted intent, sentiment score, and confidence level. After the human agent resolves the issue, the final solution and outcome would be stored in a structured feedback dataset. Periodically, this dataset could be analyzed to identify common scenarios where the AI struggles. Verified successful solutions could then be incorporated into the knowledge base or used to refine decision rules and response generation.
The effectiveness of this improvement can be measured using several metrics, including reduction in escalation rates, improved first-contact resolution, higher customer satisfaction scores, and increased AI response confidence. By continuously learning from real-world support interactions, the AI system would gradually become more capable of handling complex customer issues independently.
